Professional self-assessment

How this portfolio demonstrates growth across the program, written for hiring managers and reviewers who want the full picture before the code.

My name is Sanjay Chauhan, and I am finishing my Bachelor of Science in Computer Science, with a data analytics major, at Southern New Hampshire University. I have spent more than a decade in BIM and VDC, and today I work as a BIM/VDC lead for an electrical contractor delivering healthcare projects across New England. The role is the usual mix for that title: planning how a project's models and digital workflows are set up, coordinating between the trades and the design team, holding the standards and the execution plan steady, and running the coordination and clash-detection work that keeps everyone building from the same information. Over the years the part I kept reaching for was the technical side, automating the slow manual workflows and pulling project data into something teams could actually use. That is what led me to computer science. I came to the program to make self-taught engineering rigorous, to understand complexity, security, and design trade-offs the way someone who builds production systems should. What pulls me forward is data systems and automation for construction, the kind of software the trade leans on but rarely gets built well. I have been accepted to the Georgia Tech Online Master of Science in Computer Science program and plan to enroll once I finish here, to keep working on exactly that question.

This portfolio is the evidence behind that plan. Building it made me look at my own work the way a hiring manager or a senior engineer would, and decide what I could really defend. What follows is an honest account of where the program took me across the five areas this capstone is built to show.

Collaborating in a team environment

My clearest experience with collaboration is not from a classroom. On a hospital or government job site, a single change order passes through electricians, the general contractor, the design engineer, and a code inspector before anyone touches a wire. A lot of my job is translating between people who do not share a vocabulary, and making sure the decision that gets made is the one the paperwork will actually back up. Software teams have the same problem, and the program let me see it through a technical lens. A productive code review, which I wrote about early in the capstone and recorded for my artifacts, is collaboration in exactly this sense. It is a structured way for people to disagree about code without it getting personal, and it is where a team's standards really live. I carried that into my database enhancement. I wrote a CONTRIBUTING document with a branching strategy and a pull-request template so a second developer could join without needing a meeting first, and I designed the system around three named roles, a shelter manager, an intake clerk, and a partner-rescue auditor, each making a specific decision from the same data. The goal was a system where people with different jobs can act on one source of truth without colliding. That is the kind of collaboration I want to be good at, and it is the same instinct that makes me useful on a job site.

Communicating with stakeholders

A lot of the engineering I respect is really communication. A schema, an API contract, a clear comment, these are all messages to someone who is not in the room yet. The program graded me on this constantly, through written narratives, READMEs, and a code review recorded for a manager rather than a compiler. The habit that stuck came from feedback my instructor repeated on every milestone: a comment should say why a decision was made, not restate what the code already shows. "AVL tree chosen because a registrar's sorted export is a realistic worst case" tells the next person something they cannot get by reading the code itself. In contracting I already write for people who read under pressure and act on what I wrote, and the program pushed me to hold technical writing to that same bar. The clearest example is the OpenAPI contract my database service generates. It lets a front-end and a back-end developer build against the same agreement from separate repositories, which is communication doing structural work instead of sitting in a document nobody opens.

Data structures and algorithms

This is the area where the program added the most to what I already did. I had been writing automation scripts for years, but the formal theory behind the data structures was where I had real ground to gain. The program moved me from picking a data structure because it worked, to picking one because I understood what it costs. I can reason now about why an unbalanced binary search tree degrades to linear time on sorted input, why a self-balancing tree fixes that with a guarantee, and why a depth-first search with three-color marking finds a cycle in time proportional to the graph instead of something worse. The bigger lesson was to look at the real data before defending a design. In one enhancement my first draft claimed an input file would degrade the tree, and when I actually opened the file, the claim was wrong. Catching that before writing code instead of after is what the algorithms work was really teaching. I do not think of algorithms as an academic subject anymore. It is the part of engineering where being careless costs you the most.

Software engineering and database

This is the center of where I want to work. Over the program I went from writing single-file programs to building layered systems where each part has one job and stays out of parts it has no business touching. The Single Responsibility Principle is the idea I lean on hardest: core logic separate from routing, validation separate from storage, so a change in one place does not ripple through everything and so two people can work in parallel without stepping on each other. My iOS background gave me an early feel for separating a view from the logic behind it, and the program generalized that into something I can apply to a backend service or a database layer. On the database side it took me from connecting to a database to actually designing one, choosing indexes on purpose, validating documents at the storage layer so bad data never gets in, and thinking about the shape of the data before committing to an engine. I have come to believe storage decisions are where most systems quietly live or die, which is a big part of why I want to specialize there.

Security

Security runs through everything above, and it is the area the program changed me on the most. I stopped treating it as a feature you bolt on at the end. It is a way of reading your own design and asking where an attacker, or just a careless caller, could get in. In practice that meant keeping secrets out of source code and refusing to start the service if they are missing, pinning the JWT algorithm because leaving it open is a known attack, hashing passwords with a real cost factor, validating every input on the way in, rate-limiting the login endpoint, and routing every write through one audited chokepoint so a missing audit entry shows up in code review instead of after a breach. The part I am proudest of is being honest about the limits of my own claims. When I built role-based access, I made the narrative say plainly that enforcement happens at the application layer and not the database tier, because the alternative was claiming protection that was not actually there. I already work on healthcare and government jobs where a quiet failure is expensive, so a security mindset is not abstract for me. It decides whether a system is one I would put my name on.

How the artifacts fit together

The three artifacts that follow are one career told from three angles. Each started as something I submitted earlier in the program, and each was rebuilt during this capstone to demonstrate one of the three core areas, while together they cover all five course outcomes.

The first is the software design and engineering enhancement. It began as an Android inventory app from my mobile programming course, with a fake login, no database, and screens that showed nothing. I rebuilt it as a full-stack web application: a React front end, a Node and Express backend, an embedded SQLite database, real authentication with hashed passwords and tokens, role-based access enforced on the server, parameterized queries, and validated input. It shows I can take a stub and turn it into a working, secured product across every layer.

The second is the algorithms and data structures enhancement. It began as a C++ console advisor that loaded courses into an unbalanced tree with no validation. I replaced the engine with a self-balancing AVL tree and added a directed prerequisite graph with cycle detection by depth-first search, keeping the exact menu the user sees while making the structure underneath provably efficient and resistant to bad catalogs. It is the artifact where the trade-offs are most explicit, with the time complexity of each choice written down.

The third is the database enhancement, and it is closest to where I am headed. It began as a Jupyter notebook with hard-coded credentials, no indexes, and no validation, querying ten thousand animal records. I rebuilt it as a FastAPI service on MongoDB with schema validation at the storage layer, deliberate indexing including a geospatial index, an audit log with secret redaction, and a written argument for why a document database fit this data better than a relational one. It is where security, database design, and collaboration come together in a single system.

Read together, these answer the question an employer is really asking. Not whether I can write code, but whether I can take a broken system, work out exactly why it is broken, and rebuild it into something I would defend in a review. That is the work I want to do for the construction industry, and this is where I start making that case.
